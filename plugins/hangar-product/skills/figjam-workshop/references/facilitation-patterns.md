# Facilitation patterns

Use this when designing the agenda (Step 3). It gives you the design principles, the timing
maths, a set of workshop patterns to start from, an exercise library broken into steps, group
sizing, prep ideas, and remote/in-person/hybrid notes. Treat it as a kit, not a script —
combine and adapt to the actual objective.

## Design principles

**Divergent → converge rhythm.** Good sessions breathe. Open up (generate lots, no judgement),
then narrow down (cluster, vote, decide). Don't try to converge before you've diverged, and
don't leave a divergent block without a converging one or you end with a mess and no decision.

**Timebox everything, generously.** Every block has a duration. People work to the time they're
given, so short boxes create energy — but always pad. A block that "should" take 10 minutes
takes 15 once you add reading the instructions, a slow starter, and someone's question.

**Solo-first, then share.** Silent individual generation before group discussion produces more
and more diverse ideas, and stops the loudest voice anchoring everyone. "Write on your own for 3
minutes, then we share" beats "let's brainstorm out loud" almost every time.

**Name the roles.** A facilitator (runs the clock and the flow), a scribe/note-catcher, and a
timekeeper if the facilitator can't hold it. For client sessions, decide in advance who
facilitates vs who contributes — it's hard to do both well.

**Energy management.** Open with something that gets people talking and safe (a quick check-in
or icebreaker tied loosely to the topic). Put the hardest cognitive work in the first 60% while
people are fresh. Break roughly every 60–75 minutes. End on something concrete and forward-looking.

**Always have a parking lot.** A visible place to dump off-topic but important points so the
facilitator can move on without dismissing anyone.

**Close with commitment.** The last block is never "any questions?" — it's "what did we decide,
who owns what, by when." Capture owners and next steps on the board.

**Psychological safety.** State the norms up front (all ideas welcome, build don't block, one
conversation at a time). For mixed client/agency rooms, this matters more, not less.

## Timing maths (how much fits)

Rough capacity, assuming a warm-up, breaks, and a close are included:

- **60 min** — one real exercise. Objective must be narrow (e.g. align on a single decision, or
  generate + cluster ideas on one question). Warm-up (5) + one exercise (40) + close (10) + buffer.
- **90 min** — two exercises, usually a diverge + converge pair on one theme. Warm-up (10) +
  exercise A (30) + exercise B (30) + close (15).
- **Half day (~3–3.5 hrs)** — 3–4 exercises across one or two themes, with a mid-point break.
  This is the sweet spot for discovery, kickoff, or prioritisation.
- **Full day (~6 hrs working)** — 5–7 exercises across the full divergent→converge arc, two
  breaks plus lunch. Energy management becomes the main design constraint; vary formats
  (solo / pairs / whole group / sketching) so it doesn't drag.

Always reserve ~10% as floating buffer. Sessions overrun; design for it rather than cutting the
close (the most valuable part) when you run late.

## Group sizing by headcount

- **2–6 people** — work as one group. Whole group discussion is fine; no breakouts needed.
- **7–12** — split into 2–3 breakout groups of 3–4 for generative work, then report back. This
  is the most common Planes client-session size.
- **13–20** — 3–5 groups of 4. Breakouts essential; whole group brainstorms collapse at this size.
  Budget more time for report-backs (cap them — 2 mins per group, top insight only).
- **20+** — consider whether it's really a workshop or a large-group facilitation. Use tight
  structures (1-2-4-all), pre-assigned groups, and a co-facilitator per cluster of groups.

On the board, give each breakout group its own clearly labelled, colour-coded zone so people
know exactly where to work.

## Workshop patterns

Start from the pattern that matches the objective, then tailor. Each lists the arc and a sample
agenda; scale blocks up/down using the timing maths.

### Discovery / problem framing
**Goal:** surface and align on the real problems before jumping to solutions.
Arc: context → diverge (surface problems) → cluster → prioritise → frame.
Sample (half day): Welcome & objectives (10) · Check-in (10) · Context share / what we know (20)
· Problem dump, solo then share (30) · Cluster & theme (20) · *Break* · Dot-vote top problems
(15) · "How might we…" reframe of top 3 (30) · Next steps & owners (15).

### Kickoff / alignment
**Goal:** a new team or engagement starts with shared understanding of goals, scope, roles, risks.
Arc: why we're here → shared goals → scope/guardrails → ways of working → risks → commitments.
Sample (half day): Welcome & objectives (10) · Hopes & fears (15) · Goals & success criteria,
solo then converge (30) · In/out of scope sort (25) · *Break* · RACI / who-does-what (25) ·
Risks & dependencies (20) · Ways of working agreement (15) · Next steps (10).

### Ideation / concepting
**Goal:** generate a wide field of ideas, then narrow to a promising few.
Arc: frame the challenge → warm up creativity → diverge hard → cluster → vote → develop top concepts.
Sample (half day): Frame the "How might we" (10) · Creative warm-up (10) · Crazy-8s or solo
ideation (20) · Share & cluster (20) · *Break* · Dot-vote (10) · Concept development on top 2–3,
in groups (40) · Pitch & feedback (20) · Next steps (10).

### Prioritisation / decision
**Goal:** decide what to do (and not do) from a set of options.
Arc: lay out options → agree criteria → score/map → decide → sequence.
Sample (90 min): Objectives (5) · Lay out options (15) · Agree criteria (10) · Impact/Effort map
or weighted scoring (35) · Decide & sequence into Now/Next/Later (20) · Owners & next steps (5).

### Retro / review
**Goal:** reflect on what happened and agree improvements.
Arc: set the scene → gather data → generate insight → decide actions.
Sample (60–90 min): Set the scene + safety (5) · Solo: what went well / what didn't / ideas
(15) · Cluster & discuss (20) · Vote on what to tackle (10) · Actions with owners (15).

### Journey / service mapping
**Goal:** build a shared picture of a user or service journey and find pain/opportunity.
Arc: define scope & persona → map stages → layer actions/thoughts/emotions → mark pains →
spot opportunities.
Sample (half day): Objectives & persona (15) · Define stages across the top (20) · Layer:
actions, then thoughts/feelings, then touchpoints (40) · *Break* · Mark pain points & moments
that matter (20) · Opportunity stickies (25) · Prioritise opportunities (15) · Next steps (10).

## Exercise library

Each broken into steps you can drop onto the board as numbered instructions. Always state the
timebox and the format (solo / pairs / group / whole group).

**Check-in / icebreaker (5–10 min, whole group).** 1) Pose one light question on a sticky ("one word
for how you're arriving today" / "a recent project you're proud of"). 2) Each person adds a
sticky. 3) Quick round-the-room. Purpose: voices in the room early, safety.

**Hopes & fears (10–15 min, solo→whole group).** 1) Two columns: Hopes, Fears. 2) Solo, 3 min, add
stickies to each. 3) Cluster and talk to the themes. Purpose: surface expectations and risks at
a kickoff.

**Silent sticky dump (15–30 min, solo→cluster).** 1) Pose the prompt clearly at the top. 2) 5–7
min silent writing, one idea per sticky. 3) Everyone posts to the shared area. 4) Facilitator
groups similar stickies into themes with the group. Purpose: divergent generation without
anchoring.

**Crazy-8s (15–20 min, solo).** 1) Each person folds thinking into 8 quick ideas/sketches, ~1
min each. 2) Post. 3) Star the ideas with energy. Purpose: rapid creative divergence.

**Affinity clustering (15–25 min, group).** 1) Take a wall of stickies. 2) Silently group ones
that feel related. 3) Name each cluster. Purpose: turn raw ideas into themes before deciding.

**Dot voting (10–15 min, whole group).** 1) Give everyone N dots (≈ items÷3). 2) Place on the
options that matter most; allow stacking or not (decide upfront). 3) Read the result, discuss
surprises. Purpose: fast convergence and visible consensus.

**Impact/Effort matrix (20–35 min, group).** 1) Draw 2×2: Impact (y) × Effort (x). 2) Place each
option as a sticky. 3) Debate placements. 4) "Quick wins" (high impact / low effort) get
attention first. Purpose: prioritise visually.

**How-Might-We reframing (20–30 min, group).** 1) Take a top problem. 2) Reframe as "How might
we…?" opening it to solutions. 3) Draft 2–3 HMW statements per problem. Purpose: bridge from
problem to ideation.

**Now / Next / Later (15–25 min, group).** 1) Three columns. 2) Place agreed initiatives by
horizon. 3) Sanity-check capacity in "Now". Purpose: sequence decisions into a rough roadmap.

**1-2-4-All (15–20 min, scales to any size).** 1) Solo think (1 min). 2) Pairs (2 min). 3) Fours
(4 min). 4) Best ideas to the whole room. Purpose: every voice contributes even in big groups.

**Closing / commitments (10–15 min, whole group).** 1) Capture decisions made. 2) For each action,
name an owner and a date. 3) One-word check-out. Purpose: end with momentum and accountability.

## Prep / pre-work

Prep is what makes exercises start warm instead of cold. Recommend it explicitly in the agenda
and explain why each piece helps.

- **Pre-read** — a short brief (objective, agenda, any background) so no one arrives blind. Keep
  it to what they actually need.
- **Async sticky drop** — for divergent exercises, ask participants to add a few stickies the
  day before (e.g. "your top frustration with the current process"). The session then starts
  from real material and you spend live time on synthesis, not blank-page silence.
- **Pre-populated frames** — load known facts onto the board ahead of time: existing research,
  draft personas, known problems, prior decisions. Saves live time and signals the session is
  building on real work.
- **Materials & roles** — confirm facilitator/scribe, who's presenting any context, and any
  physical materials for in-person.
- **Tech check** — for remote/hybrid, ask people to open FigJam and find their name/zone before
  the start so the first 10 minutes aren't lost to onboarding.

## Remote vs in-person vs hybrid

- **Remote** — shorter blocks, more frequent breaks (screen fatigue is real), explicit "post your
  stickies now" cues, and a named person watching the chat/board for people who fall behind.
  Energisers that work on camera (this-or-that polls) beat physical ones.
- **In-person** — you can run longer, use physical movement (gallery walks, standing clustering),
  and the FigJam board often runs on a shared screen or is filled in after from photos. Make text
  big enough to read from across the room if projected.
- **Hybrid** — the hardest mode. Everyone works in FigJam regardless of location so remote people
  aren't second-class. Assign an in-room facilitator and a remote-room facilitator. Avoid
  in-room-only side conversations; if it's not on the board, it didn't happen.
